Results

Key findings and insights from the Elk Habitat Prediction System

Model Performance

The Random Forest Classifier achieved exceptional predictive performance on the elk habitat suitability classification task. On the test dataset (30% of the total data), the model attained an overall accuracy of 97.1%, demonstrating its effectiveness for this application.

Class Precision Recall F1-Score Support
Low 0.98 0.98 0.98 22,500
Medium 0.96 0.97 0.96 30,000
High 0.98 0.97 0.97 22,500
Weighted Avg 0.97 0.97 0.97 75,000

The high precision and recall values across all three habitat suitability classes indicate that the model performs consistently well for each category. The slightly lower performance for the Medium class likely reflects the inherent ambiguity in distinguishing between moderate habitat quality and the more extreme categories.

Feature Importance Analysis

The analysis identified terrain slope as the most influential predictor, accounting for 40.8% of the model's predictive power. This finding aligns with ecological understanding of elk behavior, as these animals typically prefer moderate slopes that provide a balance between accessibility and security.

Vegetation density, measured by the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), emerged as the second most important feature (13.7%), highlighting the significance of forage availability and cover in elk habitat selection.

Elevation ranked third in importance (12.3%), reflecting elk's preference for certain elevation ranges that offer optimal environmental conditions.

Other notable contributors included elevation category (6.2%), edge proximity (5.8%), and vegetation moisture content measured by NDMI (4.4%). Distance features (proximity to roads, water, and access points) each contributed between 1.7-1.9% to the model's predictive power.

Spatial Distribution of Habitat Suitability

This northern unit shows a distinct pattern of high suitability areas along mid-elevation slopes with good vegetation cover. Low suitability areas are concentrated in steep terrain and areas close to major roads.

The application of the trained model to the entire study area produced comprehensive habitat suitability maps for GMUs 12, 23, and 24. These maps reveal distinct spatial patterns in predicted elk habitat quality across the landscape.

High Suitability Areas

High suitability areas were predominantly found in mid-elevation zones (2,000-3,000 meters) with moderate slopes (10-30°) and substantial vegetation cover (NDVI values > 0.6). These areas typically featured a mosaic of forest and open meadows, creating the edge habitats that elk prefer for both forage and security.

Medium Suitability Areas

Medium suitability areas generally occurred in transitional zones between high and low suitability regions, often characterized by either steeper slopes, less optimal vegetation density, or greater proximity to human disturbance factors such as roads.

Low Suitability Areas

Low suitability areas were primarily associated with very steep terrain (slopes > 30°), extremely high elevations with limited vegetation, areas very close to roads and human access points, or regions with minimal vegetation cover.

Probability Distribution Analysis

The probability distribution analysis reveals the confidence level of our model's predictions across the study area:

Distribution of prediction probabilities for each habitat suitability class

The probability distribution analysis revealed that approximately 32% of the study area was classified as high suitability, 40% as medium suitability, and 28% as low suitability. This distribution was intentionally calibrated during the habitat score calculation to ensure balanced representation of each class while maintaining ecological validity.

Practical Applications

For Hunters

Our habitat suitability maps provide valuable guidance for planning hunting expeditions:

  • Identify high-probability areas to focus scouting efforts
  • Understand terrain features that influence elk distribution
  • Plan access routes that minimize disturbance
  • Anticipate elk movement patterns based on habitat connectivity
  • Optimize time in the field by targeting the most promising locations

The interactive map allows hunters to explore specific areas of interest and plan their approach based on detailed habitat information.

For Wildlife Managers

Our results offer valuable insights for conservation and management:

  • Identify critical habitat areas for protection and enhancement
  • Guide land acquisition and conservation easement priorities
  • Inform habitat improvement projects to maximize impact
  • Develop more targeted harvest strategies based on habitat distribution
  • Monitor potential impacts of development and recreation
  • Anticipate shifts in habitat suitability due to climate change

These data-driven insights can help optimize resource allocation and enhance the effectiveness of wildlife management efforts.
